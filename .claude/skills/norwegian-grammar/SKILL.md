# Norwegian Bokmål Grammar Reference

## Gender System (en / ei / et)

Norwegian has three grammatical genders. Every noun has one.

| Gender | Indefinite article | Definite suffix | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| Common (en) | en | -en | en hund → hunden |
| Feminine (ei) | ei (also en) | -a (or -en) | ei jente → jenta / jenten |
| Neuter (et) | et | -et | et hus → huset |

**Key point:** Feminine nouns can use either `ei/a` (traditional) or `en/en` (urban/formal). Both are correct Bokmål. This app uses traditional forms (`ei/a`) for A1/A2 to expose learners to both.

**Common en-words:** mann, gutt, hund, bil, dag, tid, by, stol, bok (irregular: ei bok)
**Common ei-words:** jente, dame, dør, bru, bygd, hytte
**Common et-words:** hus, barn, navn, land, bord, vindu, sted

## Articles

### Indefinite
- en hund (a dog) — common
- ei jente (a girl) — feminine
- et hus (a house) — neuter

### Definite singular
- hund**en** (the dog)
- jent**a** / jent**en** (the girl)
- hus**et** (the house)

### Plural indefinite
- hunder (dogs) — most en-words add -er
- jenter (girls)
- hus (houses) — et-words often unchanged

### Plural definite
- hund**ene** (the dogs)
- jent**ene** (the girls)
- hus**ene** (the houses)

## V2 Word Order — THE CRITICAL RULE

In Norwegian main clauses, **the finite verb must always be in the second position.**

Normal SVO: `Jeg spiser mat.` (I eat food.)
Fronted adverb: `I dag spiser jeg mat.` (Today I eat food. — verb stays second, subject moves after)
Fronted object: `Mat spiser jeg ikke.` (Food I don't eat. — rare but correct)

**Common V2 errors by English speakers:**
- ✗ `I dag jeg spiser mat.` — verb pushed to third position
- ✓ `I dag spiser jeg mat.`

**Subordinate clauses do NOT use V2.** Word order is SVO with negation before verb:
- Main: `Jeg spiser ikke mat.`
- Subordinate: `...fordi jeg ikke spiser mat.` (because I don't eat food)

## Verb Conjugation

Norwegian verbs do NOT conjugate for person or number (unlike German/French). Same form for all persons.

### Present tense
Most verbs: infinitive stem + **-er**
- å spise → spiser (eat/eats — all persons)
- å bo → bor
- å ha → har (irregular)
- å være → er (irregular, very common)

### Preterite (past tense) — 4 patterns
| Class | Pattern | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Weak 1 | stem + -et | å kaste → kastet |
| Weak 2 | stem + -te | å kjøpe → kjøpte |
| Weak 3 | stem + -de | å leve → levde |
| Strong | vowel change | å skrive → skrev, å komme → kom |

Common irregular preterites to know:
- å være (be) → var
- å ha (have) → hadde
- å gå (go) → gikk
- å komme (come) → kom
- å si (say) → sa
- å se (see) → så
- å ta (take) → tok
- å gi (give) → ga/gav
- å vite (know) → visste
- å få (get) → fikk

### Perfect tense
har/hadde + past participle
- Jeg **har spist** mat. (I have eaten food.)
- Participle: -et for most verbs, -t for short verbs, vowel+tt for strong

## Modal Verbs

Followed by **bare infinitive** (no "å"):
- kan (can/be able to): `Jeg kan snakke norsk.`
- vil (want to/will): `Jeg vil lære.`
- skal (shall/will/going to): `Jeg skal reise.`
- må (must/have to): `Jeg må jobbe.`
- bør (should): `Du bør sove.`
- får (may/is allowed to): `Du får gå.`

**Error: å after modal** — never `jeg kan å snakke` — always `jeg kan snakke`

## Negation

**ikke** is the negation word.

Main clause: verb + **ikke** → `Jeg spiser **ikke** kjøtt.`
Modal + main verb: modal + **ikke** + infinitive → `Jeg kan **ikke** spise kjøtt.`
Subordinate clause: ikke + verb → `...fordi jeg **ikke** spiser kjøtt.`

## Adjective Agreement

Adjectives agree with noun gender and definiteness.

| Context | en-word | ei-word | et-word | Plural |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Indefinite | stor | stor | stor**t** | stor**e** |
| Definite | stor**e** | stor**e** | stor**e** | stor**e** |

With definite article: always `den/det/de + adjective + e + noun`
- den store hunden (the big dog)
- det store huset (the big house)
- de store hundene (the big dogs)

## Common Prepositions

- **i** — in (enclosed space, time): i huset, i januar, i tre år
- **på** — on (surface), at (open places): på bordet, på skolen, på jobb
- **til** — to (direction), for (recipient): til Oslo, til deg
- **fra** — from: fra Norge
- **med** — with: med meg
- **av** — of, by: av tre, skrevet av Ibsen
- **om** — about, in (future time): om boken, om en uke
- **for** — for, too (adv): for meg, for dyrt

## Question Formation

Yes/no questions: invert subject and verb
- `Du spiser mat.` → `Spiser du mat?`

Question words (all trigger V2 in follow-up):
- Hva (what), Hvem (who), Hvor (where), Når (when), Hvorfor (why), Hvordan (how), Hvilken/hvilket/hvilke (which)
- `Hva **spiser** du?` — question word is position 1, verb is position 2

## Compound Words

Norwegian loves compound words, written as ONE word.
- kjøkken (kitchen) + benk (bench) = **kjøkkenbenk**
- jernbane (iron road) = **jernbane** (railway)
Rule: meaning comes from the last element, modified by the first.
